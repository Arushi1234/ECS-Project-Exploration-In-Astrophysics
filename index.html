What is Astrophysics?

  • Astrophysics is a branch of astronomy that uses the laws of physics and chemistry to understand the Universe. It often includes analyzing data from telescopes, spectrometers, etc., using known physical laws. This allows astrophysicists to develop new theories and insights on how the Universe works. Common focuses of astrophysics include the study of stars, black holes, and studying the way in which matter and energy interact in the Universe.

Different Fields of Astrophysics

  • Cosmology

    Cosmology studies the origins, structure, and developments of the Universe. Unlike other fields of astrophysics and astronomy, cosmology focuses on the Universe overall, rather than specific parts of it. In particular, cosmologists research and develop theories on the origin of the Universe, the distribution of matter and energy throughout, and how the Universe will end.

  • Optical Astronomy

    Optical Astronomy typically analyzes and observes visible light from the Universe. Instruments such as spectronometers and telescopes are utilized in order to do so. Analyzing light from various parts of the Universe allows scientists to learn about specific objects (Such as stars, black holes, and nebulas) and also understand the development of the Universe. Therefore, optical Astronomy is sometimes utilized within cosmology.

  • Stellar Astronomy

    Stellar Astronomy studies the life cycle and structures of different stars. In particular, it focuses on the birth and death of stars. Likewise, stellar astronomy is one of the oldest forms of science and astronomy since ancient astronomers would investigate and track the movement of stars in the sky in order to calculate the distance to the sun and predict events such as eclipses or seasons. More recently, Stellar Astronomers have used a technique such as parallax, a method of calculating distance based on the shift of an object in the sky.

  • High Energy Astrophysics

    High Energy Astrophysics is an emerging field that studies the Universe at its extremities. For example, it focuses on black holes, neutron stars, and exploding supernovae. Matter and energy in these regions often interact in unique ways due to their high temperatures, densities, magnetic fields, and velocities.

  • Radio Astronomy

    Radio Astronomy studies galaxies and celestial objects using the radio waves they emit. Radio Astronomers typically utilize radio telescopes which detect radio waves. By studying patterns in the propogation of the waves, Radio Astronomers can study characteristics of the Universe that would otherwise be invisible. Likewise, Radio Astronomy also aids in the development of technology in other disciplines. However, radio astronomy is typically used in conjunction with other fields of astrophysics and astronomy since the resolution of radio telescopes is extremely poor.

  • Infrared Astronomy

    Similar to Radio Astronomy, Infrared Astronomy focuses of light coming from objects in the Universe. However, it studies and analyzes infrared light rather than radio waves or visible light. Often, infrared astronomers study infrared radiation from objects such as nebulae, interstellar gas and dust, and young stars. This allows them to study parts of the Universe that would otherwise be undetectable in other parts of the electromagnetic spectrum.
