In The Media And The Public Sphere

Over the last few decades, astrophysics has become increasingly popular among the general public. From public disbelief at the capabilities of the Apollo Missions in the 1960s to the modern elites already buying tickets to visit space, the public interest in astrophysics has grown as a result of its portrayal in the media.



Popularity Over Time

As shown by data from the American Astronomical Society, the number of Bachelor's degrees earned in Astronomy has increased drastically. From 1975 to 2020, the number of degrees rose from just under 200 to over 800 in 2020. This is likely due to the evolution of science education in the U.S. According to the Department Of Education and the Ohio Energy Project, the quality of science education drastically improved during the era of the Space Race as the U.S. was forced to realize their inferior methods of teaching on a global scale. Over the next few decades, several programs were implemented in hopes of changing this. In particular, the No Child Left Behind program was implemented in 2001 which pushed for higher national standard and a greater emphasis on standardized testing by incentivizing high scoring schools. Likewise, it also emphasized the importance of mastery in subjects like reading and mathematics. However, critics argue that this focus on standardized assessments and providing incentives for high scoring schools negatively impacted students. In particular, teachers would start to hyperfocus on reading and math while neglecting other subject areas, such as science. Nonetheless, over the next few years, reforms were made to improve science education. For example, there has been a shift away from rote memorization and towards a deeper understanding of concepts through critical thinking.

Interest in science among young people has also been fueled by the increase in scientific principles incorporated in the media. This is evident within movies, books, and large-scale programs which have captured public attention, some of which are described in the following sections. Likewise, the large scale availability of knowledge, due to technology such as smartphones and laptops, have allowed the general public to catch a glimpse into the work of scientists conducting cutting-edge research.

Space Travel

In modern times, interest in space travel (an aspect of astrophysics and aerospace engineering) has shifted. For example, ever since the Apollo Missions (the first series of missions to visit the Moon beginning in the 1960s), the general public has become far more invested in the idea of space travel. The first Moon landing triggered a brimming international curiosity to continue human exploration. As a result, the past few decades have been spent in trying to incrementally improve rockets, software, and hardware in order to reach destinations such as Mars. However, with the extremely slow progress, the public’s interest in space exploration has declined, even with NASA’s upcoming mission to return to the Moon. Similarly, private corporations have taken over the field after government budget cuts and control has slowed NASA’s progress.


Scientific Figures In The Media

Over time, astrophysics has also become more popular within the general media. For example, in the late 1970s, Carl Sagan created Cosmos, a 13-part series in which he illustrates the wonders of the Universe and human evolution. This played a major role in sparking interest in astrophysics among the general public. In more recent years, a similar role has been fulfilled by scientists such as Neil DeGrasse Tyson and Brian Greene. Tyson played a major role in popularizing concepts in astrophysics through the remaking of Cosmos and writing many books on the subject. Likewise, he has appeared on many commonly watched talk shows and also has a podcast of his own in which explains various aspects of astrophysics. Similarly, Brian Greene has also helped popularize astrophysics but has focused more on quantum mechanics and string theory. He has written and created several books and documentaries that introduce the subjects to the general public.


Interstellar

Interstellar is a science fiction movie that incorporates many concepts of astrophysics surprisingly accurately. Central to its plot is a massive black hole called Gargantua. Unlike other science fiction films, Interstellar portrays the physics surrounding the black hole incredibly accurately, especially at a time when black holes were still a theoretical concept whose existance hadn't been confirmed. In particular, it details the effects of time dilation, a phenomenon that causes time to slow down near regions of extreme gravity. This occurs due to space and time being interlinked in a single entity known as spacetime, as described by Einstein's theories of relativity. Similarly, the physics surrounding the singularity of a black hole is also crucial to the main conflict. In short, towards the end of the story, the main character tries obtain the information about Gargantua's singularity and relay this back to Earth in order to reconcile quantum mechanics and general relativity to "save humanity". However, this is almost impossible since nothing can escape the gravitational pull of a black hole past its event horizon. Instead, humanity's future selves manage to transverse time in order to transport the main character into a tesseract. A tesseract is a four dimensional space in which time is another physical dimension and, according to what we currently know, it is not ruled out by physics. Finally, through the quantum information from Gargantua's singularity, the scientists on Earth were able to solve an equation that explained the true nature of gravity. Overall, the concepts of astrophysics included in Interstellar are incredibly accurate and the cinematography embodies the many scientific theories being worked on today, making it a prime example of astrophysics in the media.


Apollo 13

On April 11, 1970, the Apollo 13 Mission launched from the Kennedy Space Center in what was intended as the third lunar landing. However, the oxygen tank in the service module was ruptured while the crew was 200,000 miles from Earth, causing them to abort the mission. In order to do so, they performed a manual "slingshot" around Mars which gave them the velocity required to return to Earth. Although the mission failed, most people considered it a miracle that the astronauts were able to safely return to Earth. Later, in 1995, a movie was made detailing the mission. Fittingly called Apollo 13, the movie was suprisingly accurate. As a result, it became extremely popular among scientists and the general public alike.
